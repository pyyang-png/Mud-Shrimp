Does PFBA Accumulation Limit Mud Shrimp Recovery in MPAs?

Occurrences of Perfluorobutanoic Acid (PFBA) in mud shrimp breeding conservation areas on the western coast of Taiwan.

1. Introduction

Target Species: Mud shrimp (Austinogebia edulis). An economically important seafood species protected within Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) in western Taiwan.

The Issue: Despite a decade of protection, there has been no significant improvement in their abundance.

The Threat (PFBA): As benthic organisms, mud shrimp accumulate Perfluorobutanoic acid (PFBA) through sediment and water. PFBA currently has the highest detection rate among eight PFAS studied in these coastal waters.

2. Methodology

  • Sampling Time: November 2025.
  • Locations: Wanggong and Shengang mud shrimp breeding conservation areas.
  • Abundance Estimation: Grid sampling was conducted. Abundance was calculated by counting burrow openings within a 0.09 m² quadrat.
A = Nburrows / 0.09 m²
  • Chemical Analysis: Sediment and biota samples were collected from areas with varying burrow densities to analyze PFBA concentrations.
Fig 1. Study Sites Map: Wanggong and Shengang MPAs.

4. Discussion

  • Inverse Relationship: The data exhibits a clear inverse relationship between PFBA concentrations and mud shrimp density.
  • Site Comparison: Wanggong MPA shows much higher PFBA levels in both sediment and biota compared to Shengang, corresponding with a significantly lower mud shrimp abundance (13 vs. 40 ind. m⁻²).
  • Bioaccumulation: The high accumulation of PFBA in benthic tissue at Wanggong (52.9 ng g⁻¹) confirms the active transfer of these "forever chemicals" from the sediment directly into the marine food web.

5. Conclusion & Recommendations

Key Finding: Sediment environments with lower concentrations of PFBA harbor healthier and significantly more abundant mud shrimp populations.

Management Recommendations:

  1. Implement frequent environmental monitoring specifically for PFBA occurrences.
  2. Conduct focused toxicological studies on the bioaccumulation features and physiological impacts of PFAS on marine benthos.
  3. Address the transfer pathways of PFAS through the coastal food chain to formulate more effective long-term conservation strategies.
